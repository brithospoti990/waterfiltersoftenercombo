The Composite Calendar (Every System's Year, Assembled)

Four cadence bands. Every line is a what-and-when with a link to the owning guide's how — this page assembles the calendar; the component pages teach the tasks.

The monthly glance — 5 minutes. Eyeball the salt level in the brine tank (the maintenance guide's one true monthly task). While you're standing there, glance at the pre-filter: pressure gauge if fitted, housing color through the clear window if not. And keep the notice-your-water habit — taste, pressure, spots — because you are the system's best sensor.

The quarterly quarter-hour — ~15 minutes. Probe the salt for a bridge (the hollow crust that fakes a full tank). Run a softened-water test strip — the screening tier that confirms the softener's actually softening (anything health-gated goes to a lab, per the testing guide's fence). Well owners with a spin-down: flush the screen — thirty seconds at the valve.

The annual morning — the year's one real session. This is the scheduled event, and it's a morning, not a weekend: the brine-tank clean (the maintenance guide's full walkthrough owns the how); cartridge changes per the lifespan guide's calendar-vs-gallons rule (heavy-use homes hit gallons before the calendar — the replacement cartridges are the morning's shopping list); the UV lamp swap where fitted (the annual-lamp law: output fades before light does, so the lamp changes on the calendar, not on looks); and the settings-and-date audit — check the softener's hardness setting still matches your water, and Sharpie the date on everything you touched (the lifespan guide's discipline: the date on the tank is the only record that survives).

The multi-year horizon — one line each. Softener resin: a decade-plus with clean feed water. Carbon media beds: years, then a swap (the lifespan guide's translation math prices it). RO membranes where fitted: per the system's schedule. None of these is this year's problem — they're the horizon the annual audit quietly watches.

The Bundling Insight (Five Chores Into Two Sessions)

Here's this page's original contribution, the thing no single-component checklist can teach: the tasks bundle, and the bundles have logic.

Protective-relationship bundles. Service the sediment stage the same day as the thing it protects. The spin-down exists to keep grit out of the softener and carbon bed. So flush it in the same session you probe the brine tank. The protector and the protected stay in sync. Same logic annually: change the sediment cartridge the same morning you clean the brine tank, because a fresh pre-filter is the cheapest gift you can give the tanks behind it.

Plumbing-state bundles. Anything that needs the bypass thrown happens in one throw. Cartridge changes, housing checks, any task that interrupts flow — batch them. The household loses water once a year instead of four times.

Standing-there bundles. The monthly glance is one trip to the garage: salt level AND pressure gauge AND a look at the spin-down's window, ninety seconds total, because you're already standing there. Separate reminders for co-located tasks is how scatter wins.

The arithmetic this buys: five components' tasks, bundled, run two real sessions a year plus a monthly glance. That number is this page's gift to the pre-purchase reader too — if you're pricing a combo and wondering about the upkeep truth, that's it, published before you buy (the homepage says the same thing, and meaning it is the point).

The Year, Told as a Year

Winter — bridge season. Cold, dry garage air plus humid brine tanks is when salt bridges form, so the quarterly probe matters most now. Equipment in freezing spaces has its own chapter: the winterizing guide covers protect-in-place and the full seasonal shutdown. (Milky cold-water glasses are a maintenance non-event — that's dissolved air, not your system.)

Spring — runoff season. On wells, snowmelt and rain stir the aquifer, and the spin-down earns its keep: my Payson flush cadence rises from quarterly to monthly until the water runs clean. City homes: a post-winter pressure glance covers it.

Summer — vacancy season. Leaving for a trip? The bypass-and-return lines live at the owning guides (the maintenance guide's vacation notes; the UV stagnation note in the ownership section). The chart just reminds you they exist before the airport.

Fall — the annual morning's natural home. Post-summer use, pre-winter, and the weather's good for a garage morning. Mine is scheduled for October, every year, because a floating annual task is a skipped annual task. Pick your month and ink it.

One scheduling trick that ties the seasons together: anchor each cadence to something you already do. The monthly glance rides the salt-bag trip. The quarterly probe rides the seasons' first weekend — January, April, July, October, no dates to remember. And the annual morning owns one fixed month, inked on the chart. A schedule that borrows existing habits is a schedule that survives; one that demands new ones is a January gym membership.

What Skipping Actually Costs (Briefly, Honestly)

Not to scare you — to price the lines. Skip the cartridge calendar and carbon fades silently: the water flows fine and treats less every month (the lifespan guide's silent-expiry — the failure you can't taste until it's total). Skip the bridge probe and a bridged tank softens nothing for weeks while looking full — hard water's whole damage bill, resumed invisibly. Skip the UV lamp's calendar and a glowing-but-faded lamp gives false confidence exactly where confidence matters. Skip the spin-down flush and the screen packs solid, handing grit to everything downstream. Each skip is small; each cost compounds. The chart is cheaper.

Why the Wall Chart Works (When Reminders Don't)

I tried phone reminders first. They failed, and it's worth two minutes on why, because the failure mode is universal. A phone reminder arrives wherever you are — at work, in the car, at dinner. You can't act on it, so you dismiss it, and a dismissed reminder is a skipped task with extra guilt. The chart works because of where it lives. Taped inside the cabinet where the salt bags sit, it appears exactly when you can act on it. You came for a salt bag. The chart is right there. The bridge probe takes ninety seconds. Done, dated, closed.

The Sharpie matters as much as the chart. A blank date box is a quiet question every time you open the cabinet: when did I last do this? A filled one is an answer. Over a year, the row of dates becomes something better than a schedule — it becomes a record. When the softener acts up in year six, the dates tell the technician (or you) exactly what's been done and when. That's the same discipline the lifespan guide puts on the tanks themselves. The chart is just the tank dates, gathered.

One more honest note: the first year is the only hard one. After four quarterly probes and one October morning, the rhythm installs itself. My Mesa chart barely needs its own reminders anymore — the habit runs on the salt bags. But the chart stays up, because habits fade and laminate doesn't.

Maintenance Schedule FAQ

How often should a water softener and filter be serviced?

Less often than you'd fear. The real rhythm: a five-minute monthly glance at the salt level and pre-filter, a quarterly quarter-hour (bridge probe, softness strip, spin-down flush if fitted), and one annual morning for the brine-tank clean, cartridge changes, and settings audit. Bundled, the whole year runs under two hours for a typical combo system.

What maintenance does a whole house system need?

Each component contributes a small line: the softener wants salt watched and a yearly brine clean; carbon wants its cartridge or media dated and changed on schedule; a sediment spin-down wants a quick flush; UV wants its annual lamp on the calendar, not on looks. None is hard — the challenge is unifying five small rhythms into one chart so nothing gets silently skipped.

How much does water system maintenance cost per year?

For a city combo: salt at $25–160 plus carbon media or cartridges at $40–120 lands consumables at $65–280 a year, typically around $100–180 — call it $8–15 a month. A multi-stage well train adds a UV lamp ($60–100) and extra cartridges where fitted. Time cost: under two hours a year for a combo, a bit more for a train.

Can I do water system maintenance myself?

Yes — every task on this schedule is homeowner-grade. The monthly and quarterly items are glances and probes; the annual morning's biggest job is the brine-tank clean, and the maintenance guide walks it step by step. Nothing here needs a plumber, which is why service plans are optional: what you're deciding is whether to spend two hours or buy them back.

What happens if you don't maintain a water softener?

The failures are quiet, which is what makes them expensive. A salt bridge leaves the tank looking full while softening nothing for weeks. Neglected settings drift from your actual hardness. And the pre-filter that protects the resin clogs or expires silently, handing grit and chlorine to the media behind it. The maintenance guide's skip-costs are real but avoidable — the chart exists so the small tasks don't get skipped.

Do combo systems need more maintenance than separate units?

Barely — and bundling erases most of the difference. Two tanks share one location, one bypass, and one drain, so their tasks pair naturally: you check the salt while eyeing the filter, and the annual morning services both in one session. A combo's whole calendar is four lines. The real maintenance multiplier isn't the number of tanks; it's scatter — and one chart fixes that.
